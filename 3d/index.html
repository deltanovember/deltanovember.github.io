Complicity

“Complicity” is not of itself an offence. It can occur in three circumstances:

  • Joint criminal enterprise
  • Extended joint criminal enterprise
  • Accessorial liability

Osland (1998) 159 ALR 170

The presence requirement

  • Presence does not have to be continuous (Franklin)
  • Presence at the time of the agreement will suffice (Sutseski)
  • Participation could be proved by telephone intercept tapes and listening tape devices (Prochilo)

Proving agreement

  • The agreement could be unspoken (Tangye)
  • However there still needs to be ‘mutuality of assistance’ (Taufahema)
  • 'Common intention' will not suffice (Taufahema)

Withdrawal

  • Requires 'timely communication' (White v Ridley)
  • Requires reasonable steps to prevent the commission of the crime (White v Ridley)
  • After which no further 'preventative steps' required (Truong)

Extended joint criminal enterprise

Extended joint criminal enterprise occurs when a participant commits another crime beyond that which was originally contemplated

McAuliffe and McAuliffe (1995) 130 ALR 26

Individual foresight, not ‘common’ agreement

Extended joint criminal enterpise is arguably a misnomer

Joint criminal enterprise vs extended joint criminal enterprise

What must have been foreseen?

  • Both actus reus and mens rea must be foreseen (Barlow)
  • Generally, there is an option to convict on lesser alternative (King)

Must there be a foundational crime?

  • Historically, no (Miller)
  • But now, yes (Taufahema)

Criticisms and defences of extended joint criminal enterprise

  • 'A serious disharmony in the law' (per Kirby J in Clayton)
  • Joint criminal enterprise supported by policy considerations (Powell)
